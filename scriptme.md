# Promotion Optimization Demo Script

## 1. What This Project Is

This project is a promotion optimization demo for Supergoop.

The goal is to show how a brand can make better SKU x channel promotion decisions by combining:

- customer segmentation
- price elasticity
- comparative pricing
- social demand and sentiment
- historical promotion outcomes
- inventory context

The core idea is:

We should not run broad promotions everywhere. We should identify where demand is strong, where competition is putting pressure on us, where social momentum supports holding price, and where markdowns or targeted actions actually make business sense.

## 2. The Main Storyline

When you explain the app, keep this narrative:

1. First, prove the data foundation is credible.
2. Then show the current business state across products and channels.
3. Then zoom into the latest week and explain what just happened.
4. Then connect performance to events, competitor moves, social spikes, and seasonality.
5. After that, show how the modeling layers help decide what to do next.

This is the intent behind the current flow.

## 3. Short Opening Script

Use this at the start:

> This demo shows how promotion decisions can be improved using customer segmentation, price elasticity, comparative pricing, social sentiment, and historical event data.  
> Instead of treating promotions as broad discount events, the system helps identify where to defend volume, where to hold price, where to selectively promote, and where to avoid unnecessary markdowns.

## 4. Important Positioning

What we are trying to do:

- move from blanket discounting to targeted promotion decisions
- connect demand changes to three operating levers:
  - own price
  - competitor price
  - social sentiment
- explain outcomes at both portfolio level and SKU x channel level
- support both business storytelling and decision simulation

What this is not:

- not just a reporting dashboard
- not just a forecasting model
- not just an AI chatbot

It is a decision-support flow that starts with evidence and ends with action.

## 5. Step-by-Step Script

## Step 1. Data Explorer

### What this step is trying to do

This step establishes credibility.

Before asking anyone to trust the charts, models, or AI outputs, we show the datasets that power the analysis.

### What to point out

- SKU x channel weekly performance data
- product and channel history
- competitor pricing data
- social signals and sentiment data
- seasonal and event data
- segmentation and scenario inputs

### What to say

> I start here because this screen proves the analysis is grounded in actual structured inputs.  
> We are not jumping straight to recommendations. We first show the data sources behind pricing, competitor actions, social sentiment, seasonality, and product-channel performance.  
> This is important because every later screen depends on these evidence layers.

### Why it matters

- it reduces doubt about where the numbers come from
- it connects the later story to concrete files and inputs
- it shows that competitor pricing, sentiment, and events are part of the model, not afterthoughts

### Transition to Step 2

> Once the data foundation is clear, the next question is: what is the current state of the business across products and channels?

## Step 2. Current State Overview

### What this step is trying to do

This is the portfolio-level business readout.

It answers:

- how the business has been performing
- which channels matter most
- how our price compares to competition
- how social sentiment has moved
- where missed opportunities may exist

### What to point out

- product filter
- channel filter
- lookback window, especially current season
- revenue, units, average own price, competitor gap, social buzz
- trend chart showing revenue against the three levers
- channel mix
- channel and product price position table

### What to say

> This screen gives the current-state picture across the last year or a selected lookback window.  
> The key point is that we do not look at revenue in isolation. We read revenue alongside own price, competitor price gap, and social sentiment.  
> That allows us to see whether volume changes were driven by our own pricing, competitive pressure, or external demand momentum.

### Important business message

> A useful pattern to call out here is the missed opportunity case: if social sentiment is strong but we are still priced below competition, that suggests we may have been too promotional and left value on the table.

### Why it matters

- gives an executive snapshot
- shows where pressure and opportunity exist
- sets up the weekly drilldown with context

### Transition to Step 3

> After seeing the broad portfolio view, we zoom into the latest week to understand exactly what moved and why.

## Step 3. Last Week Performance Drilldown

### What this step is trying to do

This step explains the latest business movement in a more operational way.

It answers:

- what happened this week
- which channels improved or weakened
- which SKUs drove the change
- whether pricing, competitor moves, or sentiment explain it

### What to point out

- AI-generated summary
- key takeaways and watch items
- latest week KPI cards
- product x channel table
- revenue by channel
- current channel groups
- this week versus last week comparisons
- product-level details

### What to say

> This is the operational drilldown.  
> The previous screen showed the pattern over time. This screen explains the most recent week in detail.  
> We can see which products and channels are driving revenue, how our prices changed week over week, how competitor prices changed, and whether social sentiment improved or worsened.

### Important business message

> This is where we validate whether the latest result makes sense. If revenue, price movement, margin, and sentiment tell conflicting stories, that is exactly the kind of thing we want to catch here before making a recommendation.

### Why it matters

- gives confidence in the latest KPI story
- links topline movement to SKU x channel behavior
- prepares the user for event-based explanation in the next step

### Transition to Step 4

> Once we know what happened this week, the next question is what external or seasonal events may have caused it.

## Step 4. Event Calendar

### What this step is trying to do

This step connects business performance to timing and context.

It answers:

- what promotion events happened
- what competitors did
- when social spikes happened
- what seasonal tentpoles mattered
- how those events impacted units, revenue, and pricing dynamics

### What to point out

- event timeline
- today marker
- 12-month history plus forward-looking planning window
- competitive pricing trend chart
- social momentum chart
- event detail cards
- promotion examples
- competitor moves
- tentpoles such as Thanksgiving, Black Friday, Christmas, Memorial Day, Labor Day, Prime Day

### What to say

> This screen turns a flat weekly performance view into a business storyline.  
> Instead of saying revenue changed, we can say revenue changed because a promotion started, a competitor cut price, social momentum spiked, or a seasonal event created demand.  
> This lets us separate natural demand lift from promotional lift and from competitive disruption.

### Important business message

> The goal is not just to report events. The goal is to learn which event types justify action and which ones do not. For example, a competitor price cut may require a selective defense, while strong social sentiment during a holiday may support holding price rather than discounting.

### Why it matters

- connects numbers to business causes
- makes the story easier for executives to follow
- creates evidence for future planning

### Transition to Step 5

> Up to this point we have looked at the business and the events. Next we ask: are all customers responding the same way, or do different cohorts behave differently?

## Step 5. Customer Cohorts

### What this step is trying to do

This step introduces customer segmentation.

It answers:

- which customer groups exist
- how Mass and Prestige behaviors differ
- how acquisition, loyalty, and basket value vary

### What to say

> Promotions should not be designed only from the product side. They also need to reflect who the customer is.  
> This section shows the customer cohort structure so later recommendations can be targeted instead of generic.

### Why it matters

- shows that not all demand responds the same way
- supports more precise offers and channel choices

### Transition to Step 6

> After defining the cohorts, we compare how those segments respond to price, competition, and social signals.

## Step 6. Segment Response Comparison

### What this step is trying to do

This step shows behavioral differences between segments.

It answers:

- which segments are most price-sensitive
- which segments are more resilient to competitor pressure
- which segments respond more to social momentum

### What to say

> This section compares response patterns across segments.  
> It helps explain why the same promotion can work well for one audience and poorly for another.  
> The practical value is that we can tune discount depth, channel emphasis, and targeting rather than pushing one-size-fits-all promotions.

### Why it matters

- improves targeting logic
- supports profitable rather than purely volume-led decisions

### Transition to Step 7

> Once we understand the business, the recent week, the event context, and the customer response differences, we can move into decision modeling.

## Step 7. In-Season Planner Model Board

### What this step is trying to do

This is the main in-season decision layer.

It answers:

- what action should we take this week
- which SKU x channel combinations deserve promotion
- where should we defend against competition
- where should we avoid over-discounting

### What to say

> This is where the evidence turns into action.  
> The planner uses current inventory, competitive deltas, social momentum, and cannibalization effects to model weekly SKU x channel decisions.  
> The goal is not maximum discounting. The goal is smarter, narrower, more defensible action.

### Why it matters

- makes the tool operational
- ties together the earlier evidence screens
- gives a direct planning output

### Transition to Step 8

> In-season decisions are only one part of the problem. We also need a controlled markdown strategy near season end.

## Step 8. End-of-Season Markdown Decision Models

### What this step is trying to do

This section deals with markdown planning.

It answers:

- which products should be marked down
- how deep the markdown should be
- how to reduce leftover inventory without destroying margin

### What to say

> This model is focused on season-end cleanup.  
> It uses historical promotion evidence and markdown sequencing logic to land closer to zero inventory while still protecting margin where possible.  
> The idea is to avoid unnecessary broad markdowns and instead use a structured ladder.

### Why it matters

- converts historical learning into end-of-season action
- balances sell-through and margin protection

### Transition to Step 9

> After solving weekly and season-end decisions, we can look at broader portfolio tradeoffs between Mass and Prestige.

## Step 9. Portfolio Migration & Advanced Analysis

### What this step is trying to do

This step looks at higher-level portfolio movement.

It answers:

- whether volume is shifting between Mass and Prestige
- where cannibalization exists
- how saved scenarios rank across growth and profit tradeoffs

### What to say

> This section is less about one tactical promotion and more about portfolio strategy.  
> It helps us understand if promotions are creating healthy migration, unhealthy cannibalization, or channel distortion across the brand.

### Why it matters

- supports strategic decisions beyond weekly promo execution
- shows tradeoffs between growth and profitability

### Transition to Step 10

## 7. Likely Questions and Good Answers

### What makes this different from a normal dashboard?

This is not just reporting. It ties together evidence, explanation, and simulation so the user can move from what happened to what should be done next.

### Why do we include competitor pricing?

Because promotion decisions only make sense relative to the market. A discount may be unnecessary if we are already below competitors, and a defense may be justified if a competitor cuts price sharply.

### Why do we include social sentiment?

Because demand is not driven only by price. Positive sentiment can support volume and even pricing power. Negative sentiment can reduce demand even if mentions are high.

### Why is segmentation important?

Because different customers respond differently to discounts, channels, and product types. Better segmentation leads to more targeted and more profitable promotions.

### What is the role of AI here?

AI helps interpret and query the system. It does not replace the underlying data, logic, or business judgment.
