# Promotion Studio Presentation Script

This file is a fast presenter script for the full walkthrough.

Use it as:
- `What this step is for` when you first land on the step
- `What I say` for each major feature on the screen
- short enough to present live without sounding like you are reading raw UI labels

---

## Opening

Suggested opener:

"I'll walk through this in nine steps. The flow starts with current business position, then moves into signal diagnosis, timing, customer behavior, segment prioritization, in-season action planning, end-of-season markdown logic, advanced migration analysis, and finally an AI assistant that turns the analysis into a business conversation."

---

## Step 1: Current State Overview

### What this step is for

"This step establishes the commercial starting point. Before we change price or promotion, we need to understand current channel position, current health metrics, and what happens if we make a move right now."

### Current Channel Groups

What I say:

"I start by separating the business into Target and Amazon on one side, and Sephora and Ulta on the other. That matters because these channel groups do not behave the same way. Mass is usually more promo-responsive, while prestige usually needs more margin discipline."

### Products in Channel tables

What I say:

"These tables make the portfolio concrete. They show exactly which SKUs sit in each retail environment and what the current shelf pricing looks like by retailer."

### KPI cards

What I say:

"These cards are the executive readout of current performance. I use them to summarize demand scale, revenue size, order quality, and repeat-loss pressure before I recommend any action."

### KPI breakdown rows

What I say:

"The breakdowns underneath each KPI are important because they prevent us from hiding channel mix. Instead of one top-line number, we can see how the story splits across mass, prestige, and the retail channels inside each group."

### The Question

What I say:

"This is the decision frame for the rest of the step: what happens if we change promo depth by channel and SKU, and how does that affect sales, profit, and inventory runway?"

### Channel Promotions Simulator controls

What I say:

"This is the main live decision engine. Here I can change mass promo depth, prestige promo depth, add SKU-level support, layer in competitor shock, and layer in social momentum shock. So instead of discussing strategy abstractly, we can test it live."

### Story presets

What I say:

"The presets load common commercial situations quickly, like baseline, competitive pressure, social spike, or clearance mode. They help move the conversation fast without rebuilding scenarios manually."

### Live season timeline

What I say:

"The week selector makes the model seasonal instead of static. It lets us show that the right action in week 7 is not the same as the right action closer to season end."

### Live Impact

What I say:

"This is the real-time summary of the selected scenario. It tells me immediately whether the move is improving or hurting revenue, profit, and end-of-horizon inventory."

### Revenue vs Baseline / Profit vs Baseline

What I say:

"These two cards show the commercial trade-off very clearly. Revenue tells us the top-line effect, and profit tells us whether that growth is actually worth it."

### Week-17 Left / inventory left

What I say:

"This number is projected unsold inventory at the planning horizon. It is one of the most important outputs because the goal is not just better revenue today, but a healthier finish to the season."

### Competitor Price Comparison

What I say:

"This table shows how our pricing sits against the competitive set. I use it to explain whether we are defending share, holding price correctly, or leaving ourselves overpriced in sensitive channels."

### Social Engagement panel

What I say:

"This panel turns brand momentum into something decision-useful. It shows platform-level engagement, sentiment, and earned value, so we can judge whether the brand has enough natural demand support to tolerate firmer pricing."

### Season Story

What I say:

"This section connects the current week to the broader season narrative. It shows where we started, where we are now, and how the scenario changes end-of-horizon leftover inventory."

### Revenue and Profit charts

What I say:

"These charts compare baseline and scenario by channel group. I use them to show whether the move is helping both channels equally or whether one group is carrying most of the benefit."

### Inventory Projection

What I say:

"This is the runway chart. It shows whether the proposed action meaningfully changes how fast inventory burns down from here to the horizon."

### Demand Driver Decomposition

What I say:

"This breaks the scenario into own promo effect, competitor effect, social effect, and cannibalization. It is useful because leadership usually asks not just what changed, but why it changed."

### AI recommendation panel

What I say:

"The AI block summarizes the best action in plain business language. It is not replacing the model. It is translating the model into a recommendation the team can act on."

## Step 2: Data Explorer

### What this step is for

"This step shows the raw market and demand signals underneath the simulator. It explains what the outside world is doing before we change our own offer."

### This Week's Market Signal Snapshot

What I say:

"This is the signal-monitoring layer. It gives me a quick read on competitor pricing and brand momentum, so I can tell whether the market is helping us or creating pressure."

### Mass competitor benchmark

What I say:

"This is the current competitor reference point for the mass side of the business. I use it to judge whether we are entering a defensive pricing environment."

### Prestige competitor benchmark

What I say:

"This is the prestige reference point. It tells us whether higher-end retail is still supportive of firmer pricing or starting to soften."

### Brand social score

What I say:

"This score represents current brand momentum. A stronger score means we may have more natural demand support and slightly more room to hold price."

### Social trend

What I say:

"Trend matters as much as level. A rising score tells us momentum is improving week over week; a falling one tells us demand support is fading."

### Business summary line

What I say:

"This summary translates the raw signal data into a business takeaway. It tells me where competitor pressure is moving, whether social is helping or hurting, and which SKU looks most exposed."

### Products Most Exposed to External Signals

What I say:

"This section isolates external market effects only. It answers: if we changed nothing ourselves, which products would still move because of competitor and social shifts?"

### Signal-adjusted units chart

What I say:

"The chart compares current weekly units with signal-adjusted units. It is a clean way to show whether external conditions alone are adding lift or creating drag."

### Exposure table

What I say:

"The table ranks SKU exposure. I use it to identify where price position is weakest and where external signals are likely to create upside or downside even before we touch promo."

### Dataset Viewer

What I say:

"The raw dataset viewer is here to make the application auditable. If anyone asks where a number came from, we can show the source data directly."

## Step 4: Customer Cohorts

### What this step is for

"This step segments the customer base so we stop treating all demand the same way. It shows which groups are more price-sensitive, more valuable, or more at risk of repeat-loss."

### Channel Group Selector

What I say:

"This lets me compare cohort behavior inside mass versus prestige, because channel context changes how customers respond."

### Axis Selector

What I say:

"The axis selector changes the business lens. I can study cohorts for acquisition, engagement, or monetization depending on the question I'm answering."

### Visualization Selector

What I say:

"Different visuals answer different management questions, so this control helps me switch between heatmaps, maps, and scatter views without changing the underlying cohort set."

### Filter pills

What I say:

"These make the step actionable. Instead of browsing all cohorts, I can isolate high-risk, high-value, or highly elastic groups quickly."

### Recommendation card

What I say:

"This card gives the strategic readout. It summarizes what the current cohort pattern should mean for promo posture."

### Cohort KPI dashboard

What I say:

"This dashboard shows cohort size, value, and risk together. That matters because the largest group is not always the most valuable, and the highest-risk group is not always the one worth acting on first."

### Cohort Elasticity Heatmap

What I say:

"This is the fastest way to spot where promotion is likely to work. Stronger cells indicate cohorts that are more price-responsive."

### 3-Axis Cohort Map

What I say:

"This is the strategic summary view. It helps me identify the cohorts that are large enough to matter and meaningful enough to prioritize."

### Cohort Scatter

What I say:

"This view compares cohort size and elasticity directly. It helps show that the best opportunity is usually where scale and responsiveness come together."

### Channel elasticity views

What I say:

"These show whether one channel environment is naturally more price-sensitive than the other. That helps us avoid overly blunt channel strategy."

### Channel Group Selector

What I say:

"This keeps the comparison within the right retail environment. Segment behavior should not be generalized across mass and prestige."

### Product Selector

What I say:

"This lets me move from total portfolio behavior to SKU-level targeting. A segment can behave differently for one product than for the portfolio overall."

### Sort Selector

What I say:

"Sorting lets me prioritize the table by responsiveness, risk, size, or value depending on the decision I'm making."

### Segment Comparison Table

What I say:

"This is the main ranking table. It tells me which segments deserve promo attention first and which ones are too risky or too weak to justify discount investment."

### Elasticity Comparison Chart

What I say:

"The chart is the visual version of the ranking. Taller bars mean stronger response to price, and the color cue helps me quickly see whether that response comes with manageable or elevated risk."

### Model A: Historical Campaign Effectiveness

### Model B: SKU Include/Exclude Policy
